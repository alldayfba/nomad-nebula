## 1. Core Philosophy & Beliefs

### The Responsibility Doctrine

Jeremy's foundational belief: **It is always your fault.** Not the agency's fault, not the sales team's fault, not the market's fault. The founder's #1 job is to understand how everything works at a strategic level — sales, marketing, fulfillment — so they can hire the right people and hold them accountable.

> "You try to blame the agencies... you're on like your fifth, sixth agency now. That's you. It's not their fault. It's your fault. You're the one that sucks."

This is not about being tactical (logging into ads manager, building Zaps). It is about understanding **strategy** well enough to know who is doing a good job and who is not. The three areas every founder must understand strategically:

1. **Sales** — How the close happens, who should be closing, what benchmarks matter
2. **Marketing** — What messaging attracts, what funnels convert, what ads scale
3. **Operations / Fulfillment** — How to keep clients, reduce churn, deliver results

### The Knowledge Stall Theory

Every revenue plateau is a knowledge plateau. The founder stopped learning, stopped investing in new information, stopped going to events, stopped paying for mentorship. Revenue climbed because of relentless action + new knowledge. Revenue stalled because one or both stopped.

> "Think about what got you here. A relentless amount of action, an endless pursuit of new knowledge and new information. Then you started making the measly bit of money you do now. And your ass got stuck."

The fix is always the same: pursue new strategies, test them, install the ones that work. The game shifts from **tactics** (how to build a funnel) to **strategy** (which funnel type to deploy, when, for which audience) once you pass the initial growth phase.

### "Figure It Out, Then Hand It Off"

Jeremy's model for scaling anything: the founder figures out the system personally, documents it, then hands it to a team member. This cycle never ends. Most founders break because they think they can stop figuring things out and just delegate forever on old knowledge.

> "You figured it out and then you handed it off. That's all it is. And for some reason, what you ended up doing is you decided you could just stop figuring it out."

### Comfort vs. Consistency

The reason founders stall is they seek **comfort** (settling into what works) instead of **consistency** (daily habits that push into the uncomfortable unknown). Jeremy frames this as a universal principle:

- There is no "staying the same" — you are either expanding or contracting
- Hope is not a strategy — sitting and waiting for things to improve is the worst approach
- The uncertainty is where the money is — comfort zones are revenue ceilings

### Momentum as Universal Law

Momentum is as real as gravity. It accumulates in one direction. When it works for you, it takes active effort to break it. When it works against you, it takes enormous energy to reverse. The implication: when a funnel or campaign starts to falter, attack it immediately. Do not tolerate decline. Every day of inaction lets negative momentum compound.

> "Momentum is an incredible force where you actively have to try to mess it up in order for it to start to play against you. And when it starts to play against you, may God help you."

### The 0.1% Frame

Jeremy constantly references the US Bureau of Labor Statistics claim that 0.1% of businesses ever hit $10M/year, and an even smaller percentage crack million-dollar months ($12M+/year). He uses this as:
- A qualification filter (his content is for the people pursuing that 0.1%)
- A deterrence mechanism (if you don't have the drive, leave now)
- A no-income-claims shield (he explicitly states no earning potential from watching videos)
- A framing device for the difficulty level of what he teaches

### Addition vs. Amplification

When revenue stalls, most founders try to **add** new things (new funnel, new offer, new traffic source). Jeremy teaches that the first move should be **amplification** — taking what already works and making it work harder.

> "Before you go adding things... can you genuinely sit here and tell me you've squeezed every ounce of juice from the things that currently exist in your business?"

Addition is adding a new funnel type. Amplification is deploying back-end selling systems on your existing funnel to increase show rate from 50% to 70%, close rate from 20% to 30%, and AOV from $5K to $8K. The amplification path often generates more revenue per effort-unit than addition.

The decision framework:
1. First, exhaust amplification of existing assets
2. Then, create an **Impact List** of potential additions ranked by (a) revenue potential and (b) time/effort to implement
3. Execute additions in order of highest impact-to-effort ratio

### The "New Game Plus" Metaphor

From video games: when you beat a game and start over with all your skills, weapons, and loot, the second playthrough is easy. Jeremy says hitting a revenue wall is the same thing — you already have the skills, the audience, the assets. You just need new software (strategies) to run on the same hardware (your business). The reset is not starting from zero; it's upgrading your operating system.

## 2. Named Frameworks & Mechanisms

### Bottleneck Analysis

Jeremy's diagnostic framework for any performance problem. The principle: **it is never random.** When results degrade, there is always a specific statistic (or small cluster of statistics) that contracted. The process:

1. **Map your stats chain.** For a call funnel: CPMs → Link Click-Through Rate → Opt-in Rate → Application Rate → Application Completion Rate → Call Booking Rate → Show Rate → Close Rate → AOV
2. **Compare current stats to baseline** (the period when things were working)
3. **Identify the contracted stat(s).** There will ALWAYS be a difference. It is never "everything is the same but it stopped working."
4. **Laser focus on the contracted stat.** Do not take action on stats that are fine. Only test the specific area that is broken.
5. **Go deeper only when needed.** Example: if the application rate is the bottleneck, THEN look at per-question drop-off rates in Typeform. Don't examine per-question stats until the application rate itself is the identified bottleneck.

**The "every stat nuked at once" scenario:** When ALL stats degrade simultaneously, the diagnosis shifts to audience exhaustion. You've milked a specific audience pocket dry (see: Oil Field / Aquifer Metaphor below). The fix requires new messaging OR a new funnel type / conversion mechanism — not optimization of the existing system.

### Oil Field / Aquifer Metaphor

Jeremy's framework for understanding audience depletion at scale:

- **Oil Field:** A non-renewable audience pocket. Once you've exhausted the people in that pocket, they're gone. You must abandon that messaging/funnel and move to something new.
- **Aquifer:** A renewable audience pocket. New people enter the pocket daily (like spring water refilling an aquifer). You can sustain extraction at a pace that matches replenishment.

The problem at scale: even aquifers have limits. If your rate of extraction (ad spend) exceeds the rate of replenishment (new people entering the audience), you drain it. This is when "all stats nuke at once."

The diagnostic tells you which scenario you're in:
- If you can reduce spend and stats recover → aquifer (just overspending the replenishment rate)
- If reducing spend doesn't help → oil field (that pocket is exhausted, move on)

### The Impact List

A prioritization framework for deciding which actions to take when scaling:

1. List every possible action that could increase revenue
2. Score each by **revenue potential** (how much could it add?)
3. Score each by **effort/time required** (how long to implement?)
4. Rank by ratio of revenue potential to effort
5. Execute from highest ratio to lowest

Jeremy specifically warns against the "shiny object" trap — founders gravitate to exciting new things instead of high-impact boring things. The Impact List forces rational prioritization.

### The Buyer Spectrum (4 Types)

Fully documented in the VSL SOP (`directives/jeremy-haynes-vsl-sop.md`, Section 7). Summary:

| Type | Profile | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Type 1 | High-risk first mover. Buys immediately. Already sold. | Court aggressively |
| Type 2 | Considered risk taker. Has questions but has risk appetite. | Court with objection handling |
| Type 3 | High-skepticism, low-risk. Needs group-think, long cycle. | Back-end systems can convert, but be cautious |
| Type 4 | Skeptic/cynic. Time vampire. Reputation risk. | Actively deter |

**Expansion from additional transcripts:**

Jeremy describes the buyer spectrum in the context of a **stadium** metaphor. Imagine your audience as a stadium of 80,000 people:
- Front rows (Type 1 & 2): Ready buyers. Your messaging resonates. They act.
- Middle rows (Type 3): On the fence. Need more information, social proof, time.
- Back rows (Type 4): Skeptics and cynics. Will never buy. Active sources of damage.

The mistake most people make: trying to convince the back rows. The correct strategy: **actively repel** the back rows so you can concentrate energy on the front and middle rows. Every dollar spent trying to win a Type 4 is a dollar not spent deepening conviction in a Type 2.

### The 4 Quadrants of Content (for Hammer Them Strategy)

Content for the hammer them campaign falls into 4 categories:

1. **Common questions from sales calls** — What do people actually ask?
2. **Common objections** — What stops people from buying?
3. **Expectations for success** — What should they expect if they buy?
4. **Second-order questions** — Questions that arise AFTER the first questions get answered

### Back-End Selling Systems (5 Systems)

1. **Confirmation Page** — Breakout videos, testimonials, research assistance
2. **Hammer Them Strategy** — Paid content campaign targeting booked leads (30-50 short-form, 15-30 long-form)
3. **Value-Dense Email Sequences** — Informational emails (not just reminders)
4. **Setter Outreach** — iPhone selfie videos, personalized texts, content asset offers
5. **AI Manipulation Best Practices** — (5th system, withheld from YouTube — only in paid programs)

These systems are the primary amplification lever for existing funnels. Deploying them on a funnel that's already generating calls can dramatically shift show rates, close rates, AOV, payment terms, and sales cycle length without changing the front end at all.

### Content-First Ad Strategies

Two distinct categories:

**Content-First Strategies:** Lead with content, follow with direct response.
- Venus Fly Trap — Content captures attention, remarketing converts
- The Harvester — Systematic content distribution to cold audiences
- The Forester — Long-term audience cultivation through content

**Content Ad Strategies (Back-End):** Direct response first, content follows.
- Someone books a call or buys a low-ticket product, THEN gets hammered with content
- This is the hammer them strategy applied to different funnel stages

### The Hidden VSSL

From Transcript 0 ("1 Course Seller vs 10 Skeptics"): A Hidden VSSL is content that functions as a sales mechanism without triggering the viewer's "I'm being sold to" defense. It looks like organic content or education but is strategically structured to move people toward a purchase decision.

Key principle: the format of the content determines whether viewers perceive it as selling or educating. The same information delivered as a "sales video" vs. a "YouTube education video" vs. a "podcast conversation" produces completely different psychological responses.

### Pixel Conditioning

The deliberate process of training ad platform algorithms by controlling which events get sent back to the pixel. Core rules:

1. **Only send qualified leads back to the pixel.** Disqualified applicants never touch the pixeled confirmation page.
2. **State the price in the VSL.** People who convert after seeing the price are financially qualified — that signal trains the algorithm to find more financially qualified people.
3. **Never pixel the drop-sell page.** Sending unqualified conversion events to the pixel degrades targeting over time.
4. **Use IQ-based / qualifying application questions.** Questions that only qualified buyers can answer correctly serve as a filter BEFORE the pixel event fires.

**DM Ads Pixel Conditioning (from Transcript 13):**
- Run a "broky bait" quiz funnel that filters leads
- Only send qualified leads (who pass the quiz) through to the pixeled conversion event
- This conditions the pixel to find more people like the qualified ones
- Two optimization paths: (a) optimize for quiz completion, (b) optimize for qualified-only completions

### Concentrated Messaging

From Transcript 6: The strategy of compressing what would normally be 5 months of organic content consumption into 2-3 days through paid ads. Instead of waiting for someone to organically discover and consume enough content to build trust, you pay to put the right sequence of content in front of them rapidly.

This is the theoretical foundation of the hammer them strategy — it's not about running ads, it's about compressing the trust-building timeline through paid distribution of high-value content.

### Pain-Based Messaging ("Taking Them to the Pit")

Jeremy teaches that the most powerful messaging doesn't sell the destination — it makes the person viscerally feel the pain of staying where they are. The "pit" is the emotional low point where the prospect realizes they cannot stay in their current situation.

> "You take people to the pit. You make them feel it. And then you show them the way out."

This works especially well in cold webinars and VSLs for audiences that don't yet have urgency. The pain creates the urgency that didn't exist before.

### The Onion Pitch

Layered offer presentation where each layer reveals more value. Instead of dumping the entire offer at once, you present it in stages — each stage making the next feel like an obvious bonus. This is the structural approach for live webinar pitches and 3-day virtual event closes.

### Signals Theory (Cost Caps, Bid Caps)

From Transcript 3: The mechanism by which advertisers signal to ad platforms what quality of traffic they want. Every action you take in the ads manager sends a signal:

- **Cost caps** tell the algorithm "I will only pay X per result" — this forces the algorithm to find higher-converting placements/audiences
- **Bid caps** tell the algorithm "I value this conversion at X" — more aggressive signal
- **Broad targeting + strong signals** = the algorithm does the targeting for you based on your conversion data
- **Weak signals** (no caps, pixel firing on unqualified events) = the algorithm optimizes for volume, not quality

### The Trust Hour Rule

From Transcript 9 (Ben Bader tribute): The concept that it takes approximately one hour of meaningful content consumption to build enough trust for a high-ticket purchase. This is why:
- Webinars work (60-90 minutes of concentrated attention)
- The hammer them strategy works (accumulates trust hours through multiple touchpoints)
- Short VSLs alone may not be enough for cold audiences (need supplementary content)

### Financial Endurance

From Transcript 12: The concept that scaling requires the ability to sustain losses during testing and iteration cycles. Jeremy's example: $400K spent over 16 webinars before cracking the code. Most people quit after 2-3 failed attempts because they're "thinking with their wallet instead of their brain."

Key principle: The money you lose during testing is buying **certainty** — data about what works and what doesn't. The faster you can iterate (and the more iterations you can afford), the faster you find the winning formula.

> "You're not losing money. You're buying certainty."

### Authority Transfer

From Transcript 10: The process of transferring the founder's authority and credibility to the sales team. When a prospect trusts the founder (from content, webinar, VSL) but doesn't know the sales rep, authority transfer is the bridge. Methods:
- Confirmation page videos where the founder introduces the team
- Setter selfie videos referencing the founder's content
- Group chat introductions (founder introduces the rep personally)
- "Names matter" — using the prospect's name and referencing specific things they said/did

### Active Deterrence

From Transcript 17: The deliberate strategy of repelling the wrong buyers rather than trying to attract everyone. Methods:
- Qualification language in VSLs ("this is NOT for you if...")
- Application questions that filter by capability, not just willingness
- Pricing stated upfront (filters by financial qualification)
- Tonality and character that resonates with target buyers and alienates others
- Content that explicitly addresses and dismisses skeptic arguments

### Narrative Management (for Sales Teams)

From Transcript 10: The practice of controlling what information the sales team has access to. Jeremy's controversial position: sometimes you strategically withhold or frame information for the sales team to keep them in the optimal mental state for closing.

Example: If leads are coming in at a slightly lower quality during a test phase, you might not tell the sales team. Instead, you tell them "these are premium leads" so they approach every call with conviction. The self-fulfilling prophecy of "these leads suck" destroys close rates.

> "Sometimes you have to manage the narrative. If you tell them the leads are bad, they'll prove you right."

## 3. Strategies & SOPs

### Webinar Strategy (Comprehensive)

Jeremy's webinar framework is one of his most developed teachings, drawn from multiple transcripts (5, 6, 8, 9, 20).

**The Ben Bader Webinar Framework (from Transcript 9):**

A webinar makes three sales:
1. **Sell the mechanism** — Convince them the approach/vehicle works
2. **Sell the authority** — Convince them YOU are the one who can deliver it
3. **Sell the belief system** — Convince them THEY can do it / it will work for them specifically

**Webinar Structure:**
1. **Registration page** — Simple. Name, email, maybe one qualifying question. Don't over-filter.
2. **Show rate optimization** — The #1 lever. Pre-webinar email sequence, text reminders, setter outreach, content ads between registration and webinar.
3. **The webinar itself:**
   - First 60 minutes: Pure value, education, mechanism selling
   - Trust hour rule: You need ~60 minutes of real content before the pitch
   - Transition to pitch: Natural bridge from "here's what works" to "here's how I can help you implement it"
   - The pitch: Onion pitch structure — layer by layer
   - Q&A section: The REAL selling happens in Q&A — every question answered is an objection handled publicly
4. **Post-webinar:** Replay available for 48-72 hours, email sequence with urgency, setter follow-up

**Key Webinar Stats:**
- Pre-framing is everything — the quality of the registrant determines the close rate
- "Ugly slides" outperform designed slides — white background, black text, minimal
- The Q&A is where the money is — Jeremy says most of his webinar revenue comes from people who stayed for Q&A

**Webinar Scheduling (from Transcript 20 — 2026 Roadmap):**
- **Weekly cold audience webinars** — Excluding all warm audiences. True cold traffic.
- **Monthly warm audience webinar** — All warm audiences combined. Only once/month to avoid fatigue.
- Cold webinars are HARDER — but mastering them makes warm webinars feel like layups
- "A Kobe to Shaq alley-oop" — that's how easy warm webinars feel after mastering cold

**Webinar as Scaling Lever:**
From Transcript 8 (mastermind recap): 8 out of 9 members who cracked million-dollar months used webinars as a primary or secondary conversion mechanism. Webinars are the single most correlated funnel type with 7-figure months across Jeremy's client base.

**Normalization of Big Numbers in Webinars:**
Jeremy teaches that you should normalize the revenue numbers you discuss. If your goal is $1M months, talk about $1M months casually and frequently. The audience needs to see that number as achievable, not aspirational. This is done through:
- Showing case studies of people at that level
- Discussing your own revenue at that level matter-of-factly
- Making the number feel like a natural next step, not a quantum leap

### Call Funnel Strategy

The VSL Call Funnel is fully documented in `directives/jeremy-haynes-vsl-sop.md`. Additional strategic insights from the broader transcripts:

**Single vs. Two-Call Close:**
- Single-call close: More efficient, higher AOV per close, works for offers under ~$25K
- Two-call close: Required for $25K+ offers in most markets. First call = discovery + qualification. Second call = presentation + close.
- Stats chain for two-call: Show Rate 1 → Close to Call 2 → Show Rate 2 → Close Rate 2

**Call Funnel Ceiling:**
Every call funnel has a ceiling. When you hit it (all stats optimized, spend maxed before audience depletion), you have two choices:
1. Deploy a NEW funnel type alongside it (webinar, low-ticket-to-high-ticket, DM ads)
2. Test new messaging on the same funnel to access a different audience pocket

### Low-Ticket-to-High-Ticket Funnel

From Transcript 20:
- Sell something cheap ($27-$497) that delivers a fast result
- Setters hold buyers accountable to activate and get that result
- Once they get the result, setter introduces them to a closer for the high-ticket upsell ($3K-$50K+)
- Ideally, the low-ticket front end breaks even or is profitable on its own
- The high-ticket upsell is pure margin

This funnel type is specifically cited as one of the three daily lead flow mechanisms alongside call funnels and DM ads.

### DM Ads Strategy

From Transcript 13:
- DM ads send people directly into a messaging conversation (Instagram DM, Facebook Messenger)
- Works well as a supplement to other funnels ("chunk method") but rarely scales to $1M/month on its own
- Best when paired with AI setters or human setters who can qualify and tee up sales calls

**Broky Bait Quiz Funnel (for DM Ads):**
1. Run a quiz-style ad that attracts broadly
2. Quiz questions filter for financial qualification, problem severity, readiness
3. Qualified leads get sent to the pixeled conversion event
4. Unqualified leads get a different path (free content, lower-tier offer)
5. The pixel learns to find more people like the qualified ones

**Two Optimization Paths:**
1. Optimize for all quiz completions (more volume, less quality)
2. Optimize for qualified-only completions (less volume, higher quality)

Test both. Path 2 typically wins long-term as the pixel matures.

### 3-Day Virtual Event / Challenge Funnel

From Transcript 20 (2026 Roadmap):
- Massive revenue driver — Jeremy's clients typically generate "a few million" per event
- Recommended frequency: **once per quarter** (January, April, July, October masterminds align)
- 60-day promotional cycle minimum (most people promote too short and get mediocre attendance)
- Revenue model: charge for tickets + sell upsells + close high-ticket ($10K-$50K) on day 3
- Many go into the event already profitable from ticket sales alone
- Entire playlist on Jeremy's channel dedicated to this funnel type

**Key Insight:** Challenge funnels are exhausting to execute. The revenue payoff must justify the effort. For people already at scale, "a couple hundred grand" from a poorly promoted event isn't worth the effort — that's why the 60-day promotional cycle matters.

### The 2026 Annual Revenue Roadmap

From Transcript 20, Jeremy maps out the annual plan his million-dollar-month clients follow:

**January:**
- Crank ad spend through the roof (people are back, motivated, buying)
- Daily lead flow running: call funnels + low-ticket-to-high-ticket + DM ads + weekly live webinars
- Retest anything from Q4 that was inconclusive (Q4 tests are unreliable due to holiday inflation)

**February:**
- 3-day virtual event / challenge funnel (promoted since December/January — 60-day cycle)
- Still running all daily lead flow simultaneously
- Assess what's working, determine reinvestment strategy
- Test new messaging if consumer behavior has shifted

**March through September (The Gap):**
- Milk this period. This is the best sustained buying period of the year.
- For US markets: no significant slowdowns March-September
- For European markets: gap is shorter (March-June/July) — Europeans disengage August
- Scale spend progressively, siphoning a % of ROI into growth
- Weekly cold webinars + monthly warm webinars continuing
- Quarterly 3-day events (April, July)

**October:**
- Q4 begins. Last 3-day event of the year (October quarterly)
- Start pulling back on tests — Q4 data is unreliable
- Some businesses crank up for year-end write-off buyers

**November:**
- Black Friday / Cyber Monday — most high-ticket businesses skip discounting
- Slightly lower call volume and show rates around Thanksgiving
- Pull back spend around the holiday, then frontload December

**December:**
- Frontload ad spend early in the month
- Pull back around Christmas/New Year
- Some businesses surge for year-end write-off/momentum buyers
- Q4 test results are largely inconclusive — re-test in Q1

**The Meta-Pattern:** Daily lead flow (call funnels, LT-to-HT, DM ads) runs EVERY day, ALL year. Webinars layer on top weekly (cold) and monthly (warm). 3-day events layer on quarterly. Live events 1-2x annually. The revenue stack compounds because multiple mechanisms are always running simultaneously.

### The 30-Day Business Takeover

From Transcript 7: Jeremy's framework for diagnosing and fixing a business in 30 days:

1. **System Map** — Map every funnel, every traffic source, every conversion mechanism, every team member's role
2. **Identify Leaks** — Where is money being lost? Show rate problems? Close rate problems? Refund problems? Churn?
3. **Identify Revenue Multipliers** — Which actions would multiply existing revenue? (Back-end systems, setter training, new webinar schedule)
4. **80/20 Rule** — 20% of actions produce 80% of results. Find the 20%.
5. **Execute in order of impact** — Highest-impact fixes first

**Leaks vs. Revenue Multipliers:**
- A leak is something actively losing you money (bad show rates, broken pixel, unqualified leads)
- A revenue multiplier is something that amplifies existing revenue (back-end systems, better close rate, higher AOV)
- Fix leaks FIRST, then deploy multipliers

### Ad Fatigue Protocol

From Transcript 15: When a creative set stops performing (link click-through rate drops), the diagnosis is ad fatigue. The protocol:
1. Deploy fresh creatives immediately (not minor variations — genuinely new concepts)
2. Follow Andromeda messaging best practices
3. Use amplifiers in messaging (pain, third-party data, narrative)
4. Test new formats (if video was fatigued, test static; if static was fatigued, test video)

### CPM Management Strategy

From Transcript 3: CPMs (cost per thousand impressions) are a function of competition, placement quality, and audience targeting. Management strategies:

- **Cost caps** signal to the algorithm what you're willing to pay — this forces efficiency
- **Bid caps** are a more aggressive version of cost caps
- **Creative testing** is the #1 lever for reducing CPMs — better creatives get shown more for less
- **Exclude Reels placement** if you're getting bot traffic (single-digit CPMs + sky-high click-through rate but zero conversions = bot traffic glitch)
- **Broad targeting** often produces lower CPMs than hyper-specific audiences because Meta's algorithm has more room to optimize

## 4. Offer Architecture

### The 13 Desires (Rich People)

Fully documented in the VSL SOP (`directives/jeremy-haynes-vsl-sop.md`, Section 6). The core principle: stack at minimum two desires to create an irresistible offer.

### Offer Design for Scale

From multiple transcripts, Jeremy's principles for offers that scale past $1M/month:

1. **Single primary offer** — Don't pitch multiple things. One core offer with clear inclusions.
2. **State the price** — Always. In the VSL, on the webinar, everywhere. Price transparency conditions the pixel and filters for qualified buyers.
3. **Speed to result** — Compress the timeline. "How fast can I get them to the outcome?"
4. **Risk reduction through certainty** — Guarantees are fine but what really works is giving them so much information before the call that they feel certain about the decision
5. **High-ticket recurring > high-ticket transactional** — Jeremy charges $10K/month recurring for JIC (Jeremy's Inner Circle) with 3.5-4% annualized churn. This is vastly better economics than one-time $10K sales.
6. **Payment plans at scale** — When ALL stats nuke and audience quality drops, one symptom is more buyers demanding payment plans. This is a leading indicator of audience depletion, not a pricing problem.

### Offer Tiers (Jeremy's Own Structure)

Jeremy runs three tiers, instructive as a model:

1. **YouTube (free)** — 10-20% of his knowledge. Purpose: generate "house money" for people to afford paid programs
2. **Master Internet Marketing** — One-time transactional. 7 weeks of live recorded classes + SOPs. Updated annually. Access to all future updates included.
3. **Jeremy's Inner Circle (JIC)** — $10K/month recurring. Includes: twice-monthly 1-on-1 calls, quarterly in-person masterminds, course library, weekly group calls, Telegram chat, 35+ SOP library, Jeremy AI access. Full income qualifications and time commitments required.
4. **Half Circle** — Lower tier of JIC. Everything except 1-on-1 calls and mastermind access. Still requires income qualifications.

**Key structural principle:** The free content creates enough value that people make money, which funds the paid programs. This is not a hope — it is a deliberate revenue pathway.

### The SOP Library as Offer Component

Jeremy mentions a library of 35+ SOPs that are part of his paid programs. He explicitly notes these SOPs are "great for loading into LLMs and helping you get your work done." This is significant — he's designing SOPs to be AI-compatible, suggesting his paid members are using AI agents to execute his strategies.

## 5. Buyer Psychology & Sales Insights

### Rich People vs. Poor People (Content & Buying Behavior)

From Transcript 0 and throughout:

**Rich people:**
- Care about strategy, not tactics
- Make decisions quickly (Type 1 and 2 behavior)
- Can hear the difference between real confidence and performed confidence
- Respond to undershot, conservative claims
- Distrust flashiness ("the more showy you are, the less rich you are")
- Use iPhones (green Android texts = instant credibility loss)
- Respond to IQ-based qualification questions
- Use specific vocabulary: "net," "asset," "allocation," "diversify" — not "make money fast"
- Value time above all — direct, concise communication wins

**Poor people (general public):**
- Care about tactics (how to build the funnel, technical details)
- Need more trust-building, more objection handling, more social proof
- Respond to aspirational messaging and empathetic tone
- Need overdesigned presentations (simplicity signals "scam")
- Longer buying cycles
- More susceptible to real scarcity and believable discounts
- Type 3 and 4 behavior is more common in this segment

### Character & Environment Congruence

From Transcript 14: Your physical appearance, environment, and communication style must match what your target buyer expects from someone who can deliver the promised result.

**For selling to rich people:**
- Film in environments that signal wealth without being gaudy (nice office, quality home, Miami balcony — not a rented Lambo)
- Dress simply but expensively (quality over flash)
- Tonality: certain, direct, unimpressed by your own success
- The ad platform's algorithm analyzes visual environment — Tai Lopez insight: objects in frame influence audience matching

**For selling to general public:**
- Match the aspirational character they want to become
- More expressive, more empathetic, more relatable
- Environment should be aspirational but achievable-looking

### IQ-Based Application Questions

From Transcript 14: Instead of asking generic "how much do you make?" questions, ask questions that only someone with genuine domain knowledge and financial capability could answer correctly. This:
- Filters for intelligence and qualification simultaneously
- Creates a more dignified buying experience for rich prospects
- Produces higher-quality pixel events (qualified people completing the application)
- Examples: "What is your current EBITDA?" / "What percentage of revenue is allocated to customer acquisition?" / "What is your annualized churn rate?"

### Words Rich People Use

From Transcript 14: Language signals audience. If your copy uses words that rich people don't use, they'll bounce. If it uses their vocabulary, they'll engage.

- "Revenue" not "income"
- "Net profit" not "make money"
- "Allocation" not "spending"
- "Optimize" not "improve"
- "Scale" not "grow"
- "Acquisition cost" not "what ads cost"
- "Churn" not "people leaving"

### The Law of Association

From Transcript 14: Rich people buy from people they perceive as their peers or slightly above them. Every touchpoint must reinforce the association: "This person operates at my level or higher." If any element breaks that association (cheap design, grammar errors, unpolished audio, inconsistent messaging), the trust breaks and they leave.

### Sales Team Management

From Transcript 10:

**Authority Transfer:**
- The founder must be the one who builds trust (through content, VSL, webinar)
- The sales rep inherits that trust through deliberate handoff
- Methods: founder introduces rep by name in content, rep references founder's specific teachings, group chat introductions

**Names Matter:**
- Use the prospect's first name consistently
- Personalize every touchpoint (reference their application answers, their specific situation)
- "Hi [Name], saw you mentioned [specific thing from application]..." — this alone spikes show rates

**iPhone Texting:**
- Rich people do not respond to green texts (Android)
- All setters/closers must use iPhones
- Selfie video from iPhone immediately after booking is the single highest-impact setter action

**Specialized Knowledge:**
- Sales reps who understand the product deeply close at higher rates
- Don't just train them on sales technique — train them on the actual domain knowledge
- A sales rep who can have a real conversation about the prospect's problem is 10x more effective than one running a script

**Narrative Management:**
- Control what the sales team knows about lead quality
- If testing new messaging that produces slightly lower quality leads, don't tell the team
- The self-fulfilling prophecy of "bad leads" destroys close rates
- Frame everything to the team as an opportunity

**Lying to Sales Team Example:**
Jeremy explicitly gives an example where he told his sales team leads were premium when they were actually coming from a new, unproven source. The team's close rate remained high because they approached every call with conviction. Had he told them the leads were "test leads," they would have sandbagged.

### The Disposition System

From the VSL SOP: Instead of salespeople giving "good lead / bad lead" feedback, implement a structured disposition system with specific categories:
- "Not financially qualified"
- "Wrong business model"
- "Timing issue — next quarter"
- "Already working with competitor"
- "No-show — first attempt"
- "No-show — third attempt"

This gives marketing actionable data to adjust targeting, messaging, and qualification.

## 6. Content & Marketing Strategy

### Meta Andromeda Algorithm (Post-2023)

From Transcripts 3, 18, 19:

Meta's Andromeda update changed the retrieval and ranking algorithm fundamentally. Jeremy notes most advertisers haven't caught up. Key implications:

- **Broad targeting works better than ever** — Andromeda's AI does the micro-targeting based on conversion signals
- **Creative is the #1 lever** — The algorithm serves the best creative to the best audience automatically; your job is to feed it great creatives
- **25-30 unique ads** should be running at any given time per campaign
- **Campaign structure:** Foundational campaigns (broad, interest stack, lookalike stack) + testing campaigns

**Andromeda Campaign Structure (from Transcript 18):**

1. **Foundational Campaigns:**
   - Broad targeting (no interests, no lookalikes — just let Meta optimize)
   - Interest stack (combined interests in one ad set)
   - Lookalike stack (combined lookalikes in one ad set)

2. **Testing Campaigns:**
   - New creatives go here first
   - If they hit benchmarks, graduate to foundational campaigns
   - If they don't, kill them

3. **If-Then-That Scaling Logic:**
   - IF a creative is performing above threshold → THEN increase spend by X%
   - IF a creative drops below threshold for Y days → THEN kill it
   - IF a new creative outperforms the control → THEN swap it in
   - This is rules-based, not emotional

### Creative Testing at Scale

From Transcript 18:
- Deploy 25-30 unique ad creatives at any time
- "Unique" means genuinely different concepts, angles, hooks — not minor variations of the same ad
- Test across formats: video, static, carousel, UGC, produced, raw
- The top 3-5 creatives will drive 80% of spend — the rest are testing for the next breakout winner
- Creative fatigue is inevitable — always have new creatives in the testing pipeline

### Propaganda Content

Jeremy's term for strategic content designed to shift beliefs and build authority. Not educational content in the traditional sense — propaganda content has a specific persuasion objective:

- **Pre-framing for an offer** (makes the sales conversation easier)
- **Normalizing price points** (makes $10K feel reasonable)
- **Building authority through demonstrated knowledge** (not claimed credentials)
- **Creating urgency through pain amplification** (making the status quo intolerable)

### The Hammer Them Strategy (Expanded)

Beyond the basics in the VSL SOP:

**Volume requirements:** 30-50 short-form pieces, 15-30 long-form pieces. This is not optional — it's the minimum to create the "concentrated messaging" effect.

**Deployment triggers:**
- Activate after someone books a call (standard)
- Can also activate after someone registers for a webinar (pre-show)
- Can activate after someone buys low-ticket (pre-upsell)

**The Tornado:** When ALL hammer them campaigns are deployed simultaneously at ALL funnel stages. Maximum saturation. Jeremy's most aggressive content deployment strategy.

### YouTube Amplification Strategy

From Transcript 2: YouTube as an amplification channel for businesses already making money:

1. Record YouTube content that addresses the same topics as your paid ads/webinars
2. YouTube becomes a "due diligence" channel — prospects Google your name, find your YouTube, consume content, build trust
3. YouTube content compounds over time (unlike paid ads which stop when you stop paying)
4. The ROI of YouTube is not direct attribution — it's trust amplification across all other channels

### Content Format Selection by Demographic

From Transcript 0:
- **Affluent buyers:** Prefer podcasts, long-form YouTube, text-based content (articles, decks). Value density over entertainment.
- **General public:** Prefer short-form, highly produced, visually stimulating content. Entertainment value matters alongside information.
- **The Hidden VSSL concept:** The best format is one that doesn't feel like selling. A "how-to" YouTube video that naturally leads to your offer > a "sales video" with the same information.

## 8. Anti-Patterns

### What NOT to Do (Ranked by Severity)

**Catastrophic:**
1. **Hoping things will improve without action.** "Hope is not a strategy." Every day of inaction compounds negative momentum.
2. **Taking random actions when stats drop.** Leads to being "way far from what was originally working." Always start with bottleneck analysis.
3. **Not understanding how your own business works at a strategic level.** You can't hold people accountable if you don't know what good looks like.
4. **Selling to Type 4 (Skeptics/Cynics).** Time vampires who generate refunds and reputation damage.

**Severe:**
5. **Taking multiple wrong turns without reverting.** Jeremy's "left turn" metaphor: if you took an action and results dropped, UNDO IT first. Don't compound with more changes. Get back to baseline, then try a different improvement.
6. **Not stating the price.** Pollutes the pixel, wastes sales team time, attracts unqualified leads.
7. **Value stacks.** Not FTC compliant, not believable, signals low credibility.
8. **Promoting events with short cycles.** 60-day minimum promotional cycle for 3-day events. Shorter = mediocre attendance.
9. **Running only one funnel type.** Funnels have ceilings. Stack multiple (call funnel + webinar + LT-to-HT + DM ads).
10. **Treating Q4 test results as conclusive.** Holiday-period data is unreliable. Re-test in Q1.

**Moderate:**
11. **Seeking comfort instead of consistency.** Revenue plateaus are habit plateaus.
12. **Thinking tactics matter at scale.** Past the initial growth phase, strategy > tactics. Staff handles tactics.
13. **Telling your sales team the leads are bad.** Self-fulfilling prophecy. Manage the narrative.
14. **Using Android for sales outreach.** Green texts = ignored by rich prospects.
15. **Not sending selfie videos as a setter.** Single highest-impact show rate action, often neglected.
16. **Overdoing design for rich audiences / underdoing design for general public.** Both destroy credibility with their respective demographics.
17. **Fatiguing your warm audience with too-frequent webinars.** Weekly for cold, monthly for warm. No exceptions.
18. **Scaling ad spend faster than audience replenishment.** Draining the aquifer. Symptoms: all stats degrade simultaneously.

### The "Reactive Spiral"

When things break, most founders enter a reactive spiral:
1. Stats drop → anxiety
2. Anxiety → random untested changes
3. Changes → further stat degradation
4. Further degradation → more anxiety
5. Repeat until far from baseline

The antidote: **Stop. Bottleneck analysis. Identify the ONE broken stat. Fix only that. Measure. Repeat.**

### The "Fifth Agency" Pattern

Jeremy observes that founders who are on their 5th or 6th agency should recognize the common denominator is themselves. The problem isn't the agencies — it's the founder's inability to evaluate, direct, and hold agencies accountable because they don't understand the strategy well enough.

```
├── Value-Dense Email Sequences (post-booking)
├── Setter Outreach (selfie videos, personalized texts)
└── Confirmation Page (breakout videos, testimonials, research help)
```

### The Diagnostic Flowchart

```
Revenue dropped?
├── One stat contracted → Bottleneck Analysis → Fix ONLY that stat
├── All stats contracted → Audience Depletion
│   ├── Reduce spend → Stats recover? → Aquifer (adjust extraction rate)
│   └── Reduce spend → No recovery? → Oil Field (new messaging or new funnel)
└── Stats look the same? → Impossible. Look harder. It's NEVER random.
```

### The Scaling Decision Tree

```
Current funnel profitable?
├── YES → Can you amplify? (back-end systems, higher AOV, better close rate)
│   ├── YES → Amplify first. Exhaust amplification.
│   └── NO (ceiling hit) → Add new funnel type (Impact List → highest ratio)
└── NO → Bottleneck analysis → Fix the broken stat → Retest
```
