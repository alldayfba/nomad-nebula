## 1. What a VSL Is and Its Role in the Funnel

### Jeremy's Definition

A VSL is "a curated presentation where you've organized information in a particular flow with call to actions and strategic information to help validate your claims and provide certainty to people watching it." It is not a video about you — it is a video designed to answer the specific sequence of questions a qualified buyer has, in the exact order they need them answered, without the counterparty being present.

The VSL's purpose is simple: "to get more people to identify it's for them and to get more people to therefore take the action you're optimizing for." Jeremy is explicit that a VSL replicates a real-life sales conversation, just done without the counterparty communicating back.

### Role in the Funnel

The VSL sits at the center of what Jeremy calls a **VSL Call Funnel** (also written as "VSSL call funnel" or "BSL call funnel" in his transcripts — these all refer to the same thing). The standard funnel structure is:

```
Cold Traffic (Paid Ads)
    ↓
VSL Page (Headline → VSL → Embedded Application/Scheduler)
    ↓
Confirmation Page (with breakout videos, testimonials, hammer them sequence)
    ↓
Sales Call
    ↓
Close
```

The page itself follows a deliberately minimal formula: **headline, VSL, and application embedded right onto the page.** Jeremy is emphatic: "We still to this day follow headline, VSSL, and application embedded right onto the page or the button when we are running VSSL call funnels." Anything else on the page competes with the VSL for the viewer's attention and reduces play rate.

### Why People Actually Watch (or Don't)

Jeremy's core insight on viewer psychology: "When people go to watch anything on your page, what they're looking for is their own efficiency and energy conservation. They want to be able to get to the point of concluding whether it's for them or not and whether they want to take an action or not in as little time invested as possible. They don't give a [expletive] about you at all."

People are not optimizing to watch your VSL. They are optimizing to decide, as fast as possible, whether it is or isn't for them. A gradual declining retention graph is normal and acceptable. A steep early drop-off signals a broken opener.

The VSL is not a webinar replacement — it is a low-friction, always-on conversion mechanism that feeds qualified, pre-framed leads into a sales call process.

## 2. The Exact VSL Script Structure — Every Section in Order

Jeremy teaches a six-step VSL flow modeled on a real sales conversation. Every step answers the question the buyer has in their head at that exact moment. If you skip a step or answer the questions out of sequence, viewers mentally "freeze" on their unanswered question and stop absorbing everything after it.

> **Jeremy's principle:** "The perfect VSSL ideally is just a flow like a normal sales conversation but done without the counterparty communicating with you — that's all it really is."

### Step 0: The Summary Section (2026 Pro Tip — First 60–90 Seconds)

Before the formal six-step script begins, Jeremy's top 2026 pro tip is to treat the first 60–90 seconds as a compressed summary of everything in the VSL: "If you treated that period of time like it needed to be everything that's covered throughout the entire VSSL but said in just this tiny little window right here, that's what you would do in the first 60 to 90 seconds."

This is especially critical for affluent demographics who will make their decision to stay or leave within the first minute. State the price immediately if you can. Then use the rest of the VSL to expand on each point.

Think of it as: **tweet first, newsletter second.**

---

### Step 1: The Hook / Opener — "Here's What I Can Do For You"

**What it is:** The very first thing the viewer hears after play. It must immediately state what you will do for the viewer — not who you are, not a story, not a credential.

**What to say:** "Hey guys, my name is [Name]. I'm going to help you [specific outcome]. If you're looking to [desire], I'm your [person/company]. Pay attention to this VSL — I'll cover all the details."

**Why:** "When people press play and just immediately leave, it's because your opener sucks and you're not immediately telling people why they should stick around."

**For affluent demographics:** Short, direct, punchy. No elaboration in the opener. They want to know in five seconds if this is worth their time.

**For general public:** You can be a little more elaborate and empathetic in the explanation of what you're there to do.

**What breaks the opener:**
- Starting with a story about yourself
- Starting with credentials ("As seen on Forbes...")
- Asking a rhetorical question that delays the payoff
- Tapping into a minority hook (only a small slice of the audience sees themselves in it — they all leave)

---

### Step 2: Credibility / Authority — "Here's Why I Can Help You With That"

**What it is:** The immediate follow-up to the hook. The viewer's next mental question is always: "Okay, but why are YOU the one to help me with this?"

**What NOT to say:** "I've been featured in Forbes, Fox News, Entrepreneur Magazine..." Jeremy explicitly calls this out as not how real humans talk and not what convinces anyone. "Nobody would actually care about that in real life. Nobody would respond positively to that in real life."

**What TO say:** Real, specific, believable reasons grounded in actual experience. Jeremy's example of how he does it: talk about decade-long experience, spending millions on ad spend, real client results (without making up numbers), documented proof of refund and dispute rates ("2,117 high-ticket clients, 0.57% dispute and refund rate"), and specific case examples.

**This is also where you plant preemptive objection handlers.** If there's a common trust issue, address it here. If there's a money justification issue, address it here: "Over the last 10 years we've had X clients... our refund rate is Y%..." weave it into the authority narrative.

**For affluent demographics:** Very direct. A lot of certainty in tone. Authentic authority — "you can hear when somebody's talking about something they actually know." Do not try to sound impressive; try to sound real.

**For general public:** More empathetic. More trust assets embedded. Character matters — do not come across as too aggressive or too assertive (it creates skepticism in the general public, even if it builds trust with affluent buyers).

---

### Step 3: The Buying Motives — "Here's Why People Like You Buy This"

**What it is:** The longest section of the VSL. After establishing what you do and why you can do it, you now sell the bigger picture — the market, the reasons people buy, the problems and desires that drive purchase decisions.

Jeremy calls these **"buying motives"** and teaches the **majority hook → minority hook cascade:**

- Start with the dominant reason people purchase (majority hook — the largest possible audience)
- Move to the second most common reason
- Continue down to minority hooks
- Only include hooks that make sense to market openly

"When you get to this point in the presentation where you're selling the bigger picture, what you're really doing is peppering on all the reasons that people are actually going to purchase from your business."

**Example framework (window tinting):**
1. Majority: Reduce heat in vehicle
2. Second majority: Reduce sun glare
3. Minority: Look cool
4. Minority (do not market): Commit crimes

**This section replicates what would happen on a real sales call:** "Usually what would happen in a sales call would be: let me ask what's your specific reason we're talking right now? And they would say: I'm here because I'm dealing with this. And you'd say: oh yeah, we get people like that all the time for that exact reason — here's how we help that specific type of person."

**Presentation tip:** This section should have multiple slides or take up a solid chunk of the VSL runtime. Be elaborate in your explanations of each buying motive — explain what the reason is AND why people who have that reason buy from you.

---

### Step 4: The Offer — "Here's What I'm Selling You and What It Costs"

**What it is:** The direct presentation of the offer. Price included. No exceptions.

**Always state the price.** Jeremy is emphatic and non-negotiable on this: "I think you should always say the price... I always state the price up front. Always. I always will."

**Why:** Stating the price filters for qualified, informed leads who then get fed back to the pixel. Over time this conditions the ad algorithm to find more financially qualified buyers. "I am getting more qualified people who know the price to hit my pixel send that data back to the ad channel and the ad channel AI optimizes around finding more of that person."

Withholding the price may temporarily inflate call volume, but those calls are unqualified, waste your closers' time, and send the wrong signal back to the pixel — permanently degrading campaign performance.

**What to include in the offer step:**
- What the offer is (the main thing — only pitch one primary offer; mention alternatives briefly if you have them)
- The price
- What is included (explained simply and compliantly)
- **Do NOT do value stacks.** Jeremy is emphatic: "Under no circumstances do that stupid value stack. They're not even FTC compliant." A realistic, believable discount (e.g., normally $5,000, today $3,500) is acceptable for general public. Value stacks are not acceptable for anyone.

**Transition into the offer:** Use a natural transition slide or verbal bridge from Step 3 into the offer. Something like: "And by the way, this is a perfect fit for everything I just described. Let me show you what we've got."

---

### Step 5: Objection Handling

**What it is:** Immediately after presenting the offer, transition into addressing the real objections. Jeremy frames this as: "And by the way, I think this is a perfect offer. Let me go through why."

**The objections must be real.** They should come from actual sales call data. "You need to pull real data from real sales calls from real customers about why they are not going to do business with you and handle it literally right here."

**For affluent demographics:** Fewer objections, but they must be the real hard-hitting ones — the actual questions a sophisticated buyer has. Do not include frivolous objections. If you handle a real objection in the VSL, that buyer has a much higher probability to show up to the call and buy. If you fail to handle it, they either leave or show up to the call uninformed.

**For general public:** More objections, more elaborate handling. The general public requires significantly more trust-building through objection handling.

**Why this matters for show rate:** "This is the place to have the sales conversation. If you handle an objection that they actually have, they have a much higher probability to prioritize showing up and buying... If you don't handle it on the VSSL, you have a higher probability to get a [bad] show rate."

---

### Step 6: Qualification — "This Is Who It's For, This Is Who It Isn't For"

**What it is:** A clear, direct statement of who the offer is for and who it is not for. This is where you actively deter the wrong people and invite the right people to self-identify.

**Example (from Jeremy's own channel):** "If you don't have a business that has any probability at all to ever hit million-dollar months, I don't even know why you're sitting here watching this video... These videos are for the big dogs, they're for the chosen ones, they're for the people with above average intelligence, a [lot of] drive..."

**Why it works:** The right people see themselves in the "this is for you" category and feel called out in a good way. The wrong people are given permission to leave without wasting anyone's time.

---

### Step 7: The Call to Action

**What it is:** The final moment. One clear, direct instruction for what to do next.

**For call funnels:** "Go fill out the application below. If you're qualified, you'll be able to schedule a call with us. Make sure you book that in, and from there we'll get you all the information and we'll have our call. Looking forward to it."

Keep it simple. No fluff. No extra sells at this stage.

**Optional addition for general public:** After the CTA, you can include testimonials followed by a second CTA. This gives the skeptical general public buyer additional social proof before they're asked to act. Always make sure these testimonials are highly believable (captured in original form — screenshot from the platform where they were left, original video, etc.).

### Complete Script Flow Summary

```
[0:00–1:30]  SUMMARY SECTION — Compress the entire VSL into 60–90 seconds
[1:30–...]   Step 1: Hook / Opener — What I can do for you
             Step 2: Credibility — Why I'm the one to do it
             Step 3: Buying Motives — Cascade from majority to minority hooks
             Step 4: The Offer — Price stated clearly, no value stacks
             Step 5: Objection Handling — Real objections from real sales data
             Step 6: Qualification — Who it's for / who it's not for
             Step 7: CTA — One clear instruction
[Optional]   Testimonials + Second CTA (general public)
```

## 3. DSL (Deck Sales Letter) — Jeremy's Alternative Format

### What the DSL Is

The DSL (Deck Sales Letter) was co-created by Jeremy Haynes and Josh Troy (of Wires From Strangers). It replaces the VSL video with an embedded slide deck (typically built in Google Slides) on the same page where the VSL would normally live. The embedded deck scrolls slide by slide, and viewers navigate through it at their own pace.

**Jeremy's origin story:** "Play rates on VSSLs were diminishing... we wanted to take all of the same information that's talked about in the VSSL and articulate it inside of a deck. We had a thesis that there was a higher probability that somebody could likely consume more information faster through a deck that they could scroll through embedded on a web page compared to the information they would consume through a VSSL."

### When to Use the DSL

Use the DSL when:
- VSL play rates are suffering and people are not pressing play
- Sales team feedback consistently indicates leads are showing up without consuming the VSL content
- You want to A/B test an alternative conversion mechanism
- Blue-collar niches (Jeremy notes these demographics often have single-digit VSL play rates by default)
- You want to potentially cut cost-per-qualified-call by testing a different mechanism

**Do not replace a working VSL with a DSL out of laziness.** The DSL is a strategic test against an underperforming VSL or as a separate funnel variant.

### Key DSL Performance Stats Jeremy Reports

- **70% of people who hit the page get to Slide 5** (versus much lower VSL consumption)
- **45% consume the full deck to the end** (versus ~25% for a comparable VSL)
- **Show rates increase** (people who consume more information show up at higher rates)
- **Cost per qualified call decreases** substantially
- **Close rates improve only marginally** (1–3% if at all — Jeremy doesn't even attribute this to the DSL with confidence)
- **The biggest lift is in show rate and cost per qualified call**, not close rate

### How to Build the DSL

**Format the first 5 slides as an executive summary:**
The entire deck's most critical information goes in slides 1–5. "Try to keep it like an executive summary of the entire deck for the first five slides. That way a majority of that information that you're trying to get across gets consumed." From slide 6 onward, treat the deck as if the person read the executive summary and now wants to read the full book.

**Total slide count:** Anywhere from ~20 slides to 50+ slides depending on offer complexity.

**Deck source options:**
1. Transcribe your existing VSL and convert it into slide format
2. Build from a Mini Webinar 1.0 template (Jeremy's generalized template for most offers)
3. Build from a Mini Webinar 2.0 template (Jeremy's specific template for make-money offers or financial return offers)

**Design rules:**
- White background with black text, or black background with white text
- No company logos (or minimal)
- Barely designed — simple is better. "Time and time again every instance that we've tested overdoing the design just flops."
- Add navigation cue characters in the bottom-left corner of the deck (a visual character pointing to the nav controls) to tell viewers how to scroll

**Page layout:** Identical to the VSL call funnel page — headline at top, deck embedded in the middle, application embedded directly below (no button — embed the form itself).

**Embed the deck dynamically:** When you publish to web from Google Slides, the raw iframe hangs off the side of the page on many devices. Use ChatGPT to adjust the iframe code to be dynamic, centered, and device-responsive. "If you don't know what you're doing and you use it wrong, it'll end up looking like garbage."

**Tracking DSL consumption:** Use Google Analytics integrated with the Google Slides embed to track how far viewers get slide by slide. Ask ChatGPT how to configure this for your specific funnel builder.

### DSL vs. VSL — Side by Side

| Variable | VSL | DSL |
|---|---|---|
| Format | Video (talking head, slides+VO, or side-by-side) | Embedded slide deck |
| Navigation | Viewer-controlled scrub (or locked) | Slide-by-slide scroll |
| Full completion rate | ~25% average | ~45% average |
| Play rate dependency | Yes — must get viewer to press play | No play button — scrolling is lower friction |
| Narrator control | High — presenter controls frame | Low — viewer reads at own pace |
| Best for | All audiences, primary default format | Play-rate-challenged situations, A/B testing |
| Trust signals | Visual trust from presenter presence | Must rely entirely on written content and design |

## 4. 2026 Best Practices Jeremy Specifically Calls Out

### The Summary Section (New for 2026)

The most significant structural change Jeremy calls out for 2026: treat the first 60–90 seconds as a complete summary of the entire VSL. "If you treated that period of time like it needed to be everything that's covered throughout the entire VSSL but said in just this tiny little window right here, that's what you would do."

State the price immediately in the opening 60–90 seconds if possible. Then expand on each point throughout the rest of the VSL.

### Video Chaptering

"In all video players nowadays, you have the opportunity to add those little options... where it says here's what's covered here, here, here." Add chapter markers to your VSL so viewers can navigate to the sections they care about. Jeremy explicitly says most people are not going to watch in full linear order — make it easy for them to get to the part they care about, because "again, nobody actually wants to watch it in its entirety."

### Play Rate Optimization (What's Working Now)

- **Video thumbnails still absolutely rip** — Use a thumbnail that shows a specific key moment from the VSL that people would actually care about, not a generic image
- **Wistia and Vidalytics** are the two recommended video hosting platforms. Stay away from YouTube and Vimeo for VSL hosting.
- **Video thumbnail > image thumbnail.** Image thumbnails perform worse. If you must use an image, do not use a fake "press play" type image — it performs terribly.
- **Isolate assets on the page.** Only the headline, the VSL, and the application. Nothing else. More page content = lower play rate because viewers skip the video to scan the page.

### Button / Play Bar Decisions

**Withholding the apply button:**
- Can be beneficial IF you can endure the short-term volume reduction
- Requires strong financial position to absorb the temporary cost-per-call inflation
- Improves pixel conditioning by only sending back high-intent leads
- Requires a sales team capable of closing regardless — if your team needs pre-framed leads, you may need to withhold
- "What really determines this decision is your bank account."

**Withholding the play bar:**
- Only makes sense if you're also withholding the apply button
- If someone can scrub through the video but can't apply yet, you create a bad UX (they finish the video and have nothing to do)
- If the button is visible, show the play bar — let people navigate as they prefer, it increases quality of viewers who do engage

**Getting out of learning phase in 2026:** Jeremy notes exiting Meta's learning phase is currently making a "huge difference to the performance of a campaign." Withholding the button means fewer conversions sent back to the pixel, which can trap campaigns in learning mode. Weigh this tradeoff carefully.

### Length Guidelines (2026 Norms)

- **Affluent demographics:** 2–15 minutes. Never more than 15 minutes. The shorter and more direct, the better. Rich people have been seen closing from 2-minute VSLs with no additional content.
- **General public:** 5–60 minutes. The norm falls between 10–20 minutes for most offers. Some mass-market offers legitimately run up to 60 minutes (Jeremy has a current client using an unedited podcast format).
- **Current sweet spot for most offers:** 10–20 minutes regardless of demographic, with the summary section in the first 60–90 seconds.

### Production Styles That Work (All Currently Performing Similarly)

1. **Classic talking head** — Presenter on camera, speaking directly to viewer
2. **Side-by-side** — Google Doc or slide deck on the left, talking head on the right, presenter looking at and reading through the content
3. **Screen recording with voiceover** — Slides with no on-camera presence. Simple, effective, especially for people who don't want to be on camera.

Jeremy explicitly states: "There's not really one or the other that's currently working best. It's honestly a pretty even mix between the three of these."

## 5. Messaging Frameworks

### Core Principle: Replication of Real Sales Conversation

Every piece of copy, every section transition, every claim you make in a VSL should pass this test: "Would I actually say this in a real sales conversation with a qualified buyer?" If the answer is no, cut it.

### Amplifiers — How Jeremy Writes Persuasive Copy

An **amplifier** is Jeremy's framework for substantiating claims rather than just making them. The structure:

1. **Make the claim** (the majority of marketers stop here)
2. **Immediately support the claim** with multiple sources of truth — not just from you, but from third parties
3. **Weave a narrative** that anchors the evidence together (e.g., a timeline narrative: "200 years ago... today... tomorrow...")
4. **Answer questions before they form** — if you make a claim and the viewer has a question you don't address, their brain freezes at that question and they miss everything after it

"Rather than just making a claim, which is what a majority of people do, you have to substantiate the claim."

**Third-party data is essential.** When you back claims with credible third-party sources (statistics, research, recognized names, trackable records), the skepticism drops dramatically because the claim is no longer coming just from you.

### Congruence

The VSL messaging must be congruent with the ad that drove the viewer to the page. "The congruency, the messaging, tonality, just authority that you communicate with, storyline of the whole VSSL" — all of it must flow from ad to VSL to confirmation page to sales call.

### Playing the Character

One of Jeremy's most repeated messaging principles: "You have to be and match the character that they'd expect you to be."

This applies to:
- Physical appearance (clothing, grooming, jewelry)
- Environment (where you film, what's visible in the background)
- Tonality and communication style
- What you show and demonstrate vs. what you just say

**The environment communicates before the words do.** Jeremy cites Tai Lopez's insight: the advertising algorithm analyzes the visual environment and the physical objects in frame to match ads to audiences with relevant interests. "If I'm sitting here in a warehouse with a white wall versus filming on a 25k/month balcony in downtown Miami — those communicate two completely different things before I say a word."

**Affluent demographics:** Dress the part of financial success and competence without being flashy. Simple, high-quality. Neutral office if relevant. "The more showy you are, the less rich you are — that's the common perception."

**General public:** Match the aspirational character they want to see. Be more expressive, more empathetic. Design your environment to match aspirational cues relevant to your offer.

### Messaging for Affluent Buyers

- Extremely direct
- No fluff, no added "yapping"
- Undershot claims rather than overshot claims ("I take businesses to a million in net revenue a month" — not "we'll make you millions")
- Authentic certainty and authority — they can hear the difference between real confidence and performed confidence
- Skip the manipulation tactics (fake urgency, artificial scarcity, value stacks)
- Trust is built through transparent, specific, verifiable claims

### Messaging for General Public

- More empathetic tone
- Believable, somewhat overzealous claims ("the biggest, most compliant within-reach outcome")
- Real scarcity works well (not fake scarcity)
- Believable discounts work (e.g., normally $5,000, today $3,500) — not inflated fake stacks
- Overdesigned presentation increases trust
- High-quality testimonials from traceable, contactable sources
- Handle skepticism head-on — assume they walk in thinking it's a scam
- More objection handling overall

## 6. Offer Construction for the VSL

### The 13 Desires Rich People Buy Against

When constructing the offer for an affluent demographic, anchor it to one or more of these 13 desires (stacking desires creates an irresistible offer):

1. **Make money (cash flow)** — Short-term ROI on spend
2. **Save time / buy time** — Delegating tasks, removing friction from life
3. **Status** — Signaling to other affluent people; networking access
4. **Quality (products)** — Longevity, premium materials, exceptional feel
5. **Quality (service)** — Attention to detail, personalized, anticipatory service
6. **Health — live longer** — Longevity investments, stem cells, peptides, preventive scans
7. **Health — better quality of life now** — Sleep, recovery, performance, wellness
8. **Assets / investments** — Net worth growth, passive income, tax-advantaged wealth
9. **Ideal family** — Education, experiences, legacy, genetic planning, estate
10. **Tax reductions** — Strategies that preserve and redirect capital
11. **Wealth retention / protection** — Insurance, trusts, wills, legacy planning
12. **Art / creative expression** — Collecting, interior design, personal expression
13. **Toys / fun** — Yachts, cars, experiences, entertainment

**Stack at minimum two desires.** The more desire overlap, the more irresistible the offer.

### What Makes the Perfect Offer

- **Customer service** at a level commensurate with the buyer's expectations (this varies — Four Seasons warmth for consumer products, directness and accountability for marketing/advisory services)
- **Speed to result** — Compress the timeline. "Try to push it down to a smaller timeline. Try to get them to the result faster."
- **Quality signaled before purchase** — Give them a way to see quality before they experience it
- **Combo of desires** — At least two desires stacked
- **Risk reduction** — Guarantees, certainty, consistency, math that shows upside vs. downside clearly
- **Incentive for action now** — Not fake urgency, but a genuine reason to decide now rather than drift

### What to Avoid in Offer Framing

- **Value stacks** (inflated fake prices crossed out to show fake savings) — Not FTC compliant, not believable, not effective with any demographic
- **Overzealous claims to affluent buyers** — They tune out immediately; undershot, conservative claims convert better
- **Withholding the price** — Always state it in the VSL

## 7. Buyer Psychology — The Buyer Spectrum

This is one of Jeremy's original frameworks, built on top of Eugene Schwartz's market awareness/sophistication concepts from "Breakthrough Advertising."

### The Four Buyer Types

**Buyer Type 1 — The High-Risk Profile Buyer**

- Buys immediately or near-immediately
- Has one key question at most, then pulls the trigger
- Does not need testimonials, guarantees, or risk reversals
- Operates on immediate action
- "They show up already sold"
- These are your first buyers when you launch a new offer
- Require very little in the VSL beyond a compelling offer

**Buyer Type 2 — The Considered Risk Taker**

- Most common high-performing buyer type alongside Type 1
- Has more questions than Type 1, but still has a real risk appetite
- Will purchase, but needs specific things answered first (timing, inclusions, expectations, qualifications)
- More affected by third-party validation (a respected peer who vouched for the offer)
- Types 1 and 2 are the only buyers Jeremy actively courts for his Inner Circle program

**Buyer Type 3 — The High-Skepticism, Low-Risk Buyer**

- Much lower risk appetite
- Longer buying cycle
- Needs group-think and word of mouth to move
- Benefits heavily from the hammer them strategy, value-dense email sequences, confirmation page breakout videos
- "These are the people who benefit most from a lot of my back-end selling systems"
- Timing objections are common (paying for something else, next quarter, etc.)
- A time bomb for info product businesses — if you sell them, they may take a long time to get results, have a higher failure rate, and damage your reputation
- Jeremy does not sell Types 3 or 4 for his own premium offers

**Buyer Type 4 — The Skeptic / Cynic**

- "These people are the devil"
- Asks questions not to understand, but to confirm their skepticism
- Not in the buying frame — they are in the disqualifying frame
- "They are time vampires"
- Active sources of online reputational damage (Reddit threads, YouTube hate channels, Facebook group criticism)
- Should be actively deterred in your VSL through qualification language and application friction
- Never allow Type 4 through to your confirmation page or sales call

### How the Buyer Spectrum Informs VSL Writing

1. **Open with a majority hook** — appeals to Type 1 and 2 immediately
2. **Build in qualification language** — naturally deters Type 3 and 4
3. **The objection handling section is primarily for Type 2** — answering their real questions preemptively
4. **Testimonials help Type 2 and 3** — they need third-party validation; Type 1 doesn't care
5. **State the price in the VSL** — immediately qualifies out a large portion of Type 3 and 4 and conditions the pixel toward Type 1 and 2
6. **The qualification step** ("this is who it's for, this is who it's not for") actively deters Type 3 and 4 from applying

### The Friction-Intent Relationship

VSL call funnels sit at a medium-low friction level. Lower friction means more volume but lower initial intent. This is not a problem — it is solved through back-end selling systems (confirmation page, hammer them strategy, email sequences, setter outreach).

"I can bring in somebody that's at a lower level of intention and through information, critical information, piece by piece or that one critical piece, I can increase the intent."

The goal is not to make the funnel harder to get through (friction) — the goal is to make the information richer so people who enter at lower intent move up to convicted interest before the call.

## 8. Technical Specs

### Video Length

| Demographic | Minimum | Sweet Spot | Maximum |
|---|---|---|---|
| Affluent / Rich | 2 min | 5–10 min | 15 min |
| General Public | 5 min | 10–20 min | 60 min |

### Hosting Platforms (Approved)

- **Wistia** — Primary recommendation. Best analytics, best visualization of retention/engagement graphs, best customization, supports video thumbnails
- **Vidalytics** — Approved secondary option

**Avoid:** YouTube, Vimeo, and any other platform for VSL hosting. Jeremy is explicit on this.

### Video Thumbnail

- Use a **video thumbnail** (a short clip from a key moment) rather than a static image — measurably higher play rates
- The thumbnail should show something the viewer actually cares about — a moment from the video that signals "this is what you'll learn/get"
- Never use a fake "press play" image (always performs poorly)
- If you must use an image: show something compelling and relevant, not a generic presenter photo

### Key Analytics to Monitor

- **Play rate:** What % of page visitors press play. 30%+ is solid; 9% can still work at scale with the right qualifier headline. Low play rate usually means: bad thumbnail, too much content on page, wrong demographic, misalignment between ad and page.
- **Engagement rate:** Average % of video consumed. High engagement is mostly good — but very high engagement on a long VSL can mean viewers are confused and seeking answers rather than convinced and ready to act. "A high play rate represents a good thing, but the high engagement rate can represent a bad thing."
- **Retention graph shape:** Gradual declining slope = normal. Steep early drop = fix the opener. Flat sustained retention after an early drop = normal human behavior.

### Page Setup Rules

- **Page elements:** Headline only, VSL only, application/scheduler embedded below (no other content)
- **No button** for the application — embed the form directly
- **Preferred stack for embedded application + scheduler:** Typeform + Calendly integration. "Every time there's some nuanced thing about the way that [other tools] do it that just makes it perform less effective than Typeform and Calendly."
- **Conditional logic on application:** Qualified applicants proceed to schedule; disqualified applicants go to a drop-sell page (but this page is NOT pixeled — never send disqualified people back to the pixel)

### Production Notes

- **Camera:** iPhone or iPad (4K, 60fps) is sufficient. Jeremy shot more than 80% of his highest-converting videos on an iPad.
- **Audio:** Road Mic or lavalier mic. Audio quality matters more than video quality.
- **Editing style:** Jump cuts, captions minimum. Can add: music/sound effects, memes, visual edits. Match the production level of Jeremy's YouTube videos as a reference for feel and pacing.
- **Presentation decks (if used):** White background with black text or black background with white text. No heavy design. No company logo in the corner. Minimal — this consistently outperforms polished presentations.

## 9. Call Funnel Integration

### The Full Call Funnel Flow

```
[Ad] → [VSL Page] → [Application] → [Scheduler / Confirmation Page]
           ↓                                  ↓
    Disqualified leads               Qualified leads only
           ↓                                  ↓
    Drop-sell page               Confirmation page (DO pixel this)
    (Do NOT pixel)
```

### Confirmation Page Best Practices

The confirmation page is the most important under-utilized asset in the funnel. Jeremy describes it as where you begin the due diligence process for the buyer.

**Main confirmation video:** Short. Something like: "Hey, congratulations — you booked your call. Really excited to talk with you. Between now and your call, our goal is to get you as much information as we can to help you make a fully informed decision. By the time you get to the call, we ideally want you to be in or out. We're not trying to pressure you — we just want you to be informed. Below this video, you'll find a bunch of resources for that. We'll also send some emails. Looking forward to it."

**Below the main video — breakout videos:** Individual short videos each addressing a specific common question or objection from the sales process. These are the questions that buyers would otherwise only get answered on the sales call (turning it into an education call). Moving those answers to the confirmation page turns the sales call into a closing call.

**Testimonials on the confirmation page:** Must be from identifiable, contactable people. "People want to know who these people are and they want to hit them up." If possible, use video testimonials in original form, screenshots from social platforms where they were captured, or original emails. Do not retype testimonials with a stock photo — "that's not going to be believable."

**Address the research buyers will do anyway:** Smart buyers will Google your company name, search for reviews, look for Reddit threads, look for "scam" results. Address this directly on the confirmation page. Show your refund rate. Acknowledge one-star reviews if they exist and explain them. "Help them do their research" — if you don't, they'll find answers from your competitors.

### Hammer Them Strategy (Post-Booking Content Campaign)

Once a lead books a call, the **hammer them strategy** activates. This is a paid content ad campaign targeting everyone who booked but hasn't had their call yet.

**Content volume:** 30–50 short-form pieces of content, 15–30 long-form pieces of content.

**What the content addresses (the four quadrants):**
1. Common questions from sales calls
2. Common objections
3. Expectations for how to succeed with your offer
4. Questions that arise from the original questions getting answered

**Effect:** Dramatically improves show rates, close rates, compresses the sales cycle, increases pay-in-full rates, reduces reliance on payment plans, and turns salespeople into cashiers.

"The hammer them strategy is money. Increases show rate, increases close rate, condenses the timeline of the buying cycle, increases average order value, reduces payment plans, reduces financing needs, turns your salespeople into cashiers."

### Email Sequence (Post-Booking)

Value-dense emails — not just reminders. These are long-form, informative emails that address specific questions the buyer is researching between booking and the call.

"We do not just do the reminder-based stuff... We're trying to break down topics that people actually give a [expletive] to know about that increases the probability that they show up."

### Setter Outreach (Post-Booking)

Sales reps should:
- Send a selfie video from an iPhone immediately after booking ("Hey, really excited to connect with you — my name's [Name], we've got our call coming up at [time]...")
- Text from an iPhone (green texts/Android are perceived as bots and ignored)
- Reference the lead's specific application answers to personalize the outreach
- Offer to send a relevant piece of content based on what the lead wrote in the application

"I've had salespeople who do that and they hit me back saying 'Oh my God, my show rate's through the roof.'"

### Show Rate / Close Rate Benchmarks

- **Show rate target (VSL call funnel):** 70%+ with all systems firing; 50–60% conservative for new funnels
- **Close rate target:** 30%+ minimum for webinar-generated leads (webinar leads are layup deals); ~20% acceptable minimum for cold VSL call funnel leads
- **Cost per qualified call:** Typically $150–$250 at scale ($200–$300K/month in spend); lower is better, higher is acceptable if AOV supports it
- **Sales cycle target:** Under 7 days with all back-end selling systems functioning

### Pixel Conditioning Rules

- Only pixeled events: Qualified applications that reach the confirmation page
- Never pixel: Disqualified leads, drop-sell page visitors
- "Never ever ever ever send the disqualified people to that confirmation page. Only the qualified people go to that confirmation page."
- Exiting Meta's learning phase in 2026 is critical — ensure qualified conversion events fire frequently enough to avoid perpetual learning limited status

## 10. Common Mistakes to Avoid

### VSL Content Mistakes

1. **Value stacks** — Non-compliant, unbelievable, immediately signals low credibility. Never do this.
2. **Not stating the price** — Inflates call volume with unqualified leads, destroys pixel conditioning, trains the algorithm to find the wrong people.
3. **Opener that doesn't immediately say what you can do for the viewer** — Causes the steep early drop-off on the retention graph.
4. **Using fake press-play image thumbnails** — Always performs poorly.
5. **Answering questions out of order** — Skipping a step in the script flow causes viewers to freeze on their unanswered question and miss everything after.
6. **Overdone presentation design** — For affluent demographics, heavy design signals insecurity ("the more showy you are, the less rich you appear"). Stick to white/black minimalism.
7. **Underdone presentation design for general public** — The opposite: general public reads simplicity as a scam. You need quality design and trust signals.
8. **Being too direct with general public** — "If you are as direct to the general public as you can be to affluent people, you're going to be perceived as an [expletive]."
9. **Making overzealous claims to affluent buyers** — They immediately tune out. Undershot conservative claims convert better with rich audiences.
10. **Testimonials that aren't believable or contactable** — The most common confirmation page failure.

### Page/Funnel Mistakes

11. **Too much content on the VSL page** — Every additional element below the video reduces play rate because viewers scan the page instead of watching.
12. **Using a button instead of an embedded application** — A button creates unnecessary friction and inflates your cost per application artificially.
13. **Sending disqualified leads to the confirmation page** — Destroys pixel conditioning. "Never send the turds back to the pixel."
14. **Pixeling the drop-sell page** — Same issue. Only pixel the qualified confirmation page.

### Sales Team Mistakes

15. **Not sending selfie videos** — Show rates drop immediately when salespeople stop this habit.
16. **Texting from Android** — Rich people do not respond to green texts; they treat them as bots.
17. **Using "good/bad" lead quality feedback instead of dispositions** — Actionable dispositions are what marketers can actually use to improve the funnel and the content sequence.
18. **Hiring salespeople reactively (only after the calendar fills)** — Results in every-other-month scaling. Hire ahead of the fill to scale 12 months a year instead of 6.

### Strategic Mistakes

19. **Withholding the play bar without also withholding the button** — Illogical UX. If someone can see the video length but has no button to click when they're done, you've frustrated a ready buyer.
20. **Running only high-friction funnels** — Limits volume dramatically, especially with affluent buyers. Combine low-friction VSL call funnels with higher-friction formats for maximum reach.
21. **Selling to Buyer Types 3 and 4** — A time bomb for your reputation. Their failure rate is too high regardless of offer quality.
22. **Stopping consistent sales training** — Close rates and show rates decline every time this habit stops.

## 11. Production Checklist

Use this checklist before a VSL goes live.

### Script / Content

- [ ] Summary section exists in the first 60–90 seconds covering everything in the full VSL
- [ ] Price stated clearly within the first ~90 seconds or at minimum in Step 4 (offer section)
- [ ] Step 1 (Hook): Viewer knows immediately what you can do for them
- [ ] Step 2 (Credibility): Specific, verifiable, believable reasons — not generic credentials
- [ ] Step 3 (Buying Motives): Majority hook first, cascading to minority hooks
- [ ] Step 4 (Offer): Price stated, inclusions described compliantly, no value stacks
- [ ] Step 5 (Objections): Real objections sourced from actual sales calls
- [ ] Step 6 (Qualification): Clear "this is for you / not for you" language
- [ ] Step 7 (CTA): One clear action instruction
- [ ] Messaging is congruent with the ad creative that drives traffic to this page
- [ ] Language and tone matches the demographic (direct for affluent, empathetic for general public)
- [ ] Character / presenter appearance matches buyer expectations

### Video Production

- [ ] Video thumbnail created (shows a key compelling moment from the VSL, not a generic image or fake "press play")
- [ ] Hosted on Wistia or Vidalytics (not YouTube, not Vimeo)
- [ ] Video chapters added (Wistia chapter markers)
- [ ] Video length is within acceptable range for the demographic (affluent: max 15 min; general public: max 60 min)
- [ ] Audio is clear (Road Mic or lavalier)
- [ ] Captions and jump cuts at minimum; additional editing as needed
- [ ] Presenter appearance and environment match demographic expectations

### Page Setup

- [ ] Page has only three elements: headline, VSL, application/scheduler
- [ ] Application is embedded (not a button)
- [ ] No additional sections below the application (no testimonials wall, no FAQ accordion, no extra copy)
- [ ] Typeform + Calendly integration confirmed working
- [ ] Conditional logic on application working: qualified leads go to confirmation page, disqualified to drop-sell
- [ ] Confirmation page is pixeled (for the ad channel conversion event)
- [ ] Drop-sell page is NOT pixeled
- [ ] Play bar: visible (if button is visible) OR hidden (if button is also withheld)
- [ ] Apply button: visible from load OR delayed (decision made based on budget, team capability, and pixel conditioning strategy)

### Confirmation Page

- [ ] Main confirmation page video recorded (brief, sets expectations for the process between now and the call)
- [ ] Breakout videos created addressing the most common pre-call questions/objections
- [ ] Testimonials present from identifiable, contactable real clients
- [ ] Company search + scam/review handling addressed somewhere on the confirmation page
- [ ] Refund/dispute rate data shown if applicable (especially for info products)

### Back-End Selling Systems

- [ ] Hammer them campaign ready to launch targeting post-booking leads (30–50 short form, 15–30 long form pieces of content)
- [ ] Value-dense email sequence live (not just reminders — actual informational emails)
- [ ] Setter best practices documented and trained: selfie video, iPhone texting, personalized reference to application, content asset offer
- [ ] Disposition system in place so sales team can give structured feedback (not just "leads are good/bad")

### Analytics

- [ ] Wistia analytics monitoring set up (play rate dashboard, engagement/retention graph)
- [ ] Pixel firing correctly on the confirmation page
- [ ] Pixel NOT firing on the drop-sell page
- [ ] Cost per qualified call tracked
- [ ] Show rate tracked
- [ ] Close rate tracked
- [ ] Weekly bottleneck analysis process in place (identify which stat is contracted and attack it)
